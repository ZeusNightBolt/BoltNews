## Rates & Fed: Warsh's Quiet Revolution

The most underappreciated story in markets: **Kevin Warsh is preparing to fundamentally restructure how the Fed manages its $6.8 trillion balance sheet.** This isn't about rate cuts vs. hikes — it's about the entire operating framework.

Key data points from deep extraction of CNBC's analysis:
- Fed balance sheet: $6.8T (23% of GDP) — 7x the pre-2008 share
- Fed research suggests up to **$2.1 trillion in cuts** are possible under new framework
- The overnight repo rate could **replace the federal funds rate** as the primary policy transmission mechanism
- This would allow a surface-level "lower rate" to satisfy political pressure while keeping underlying financing tight

**The fault lines:**
- Governor Barr: "Shrinking the balance sheet is the wrong objective... would undermine bank resilience, impede money market functioning, threaten financial stability"
- Dallas Fed's Logan: Cautious — "any changes should be gradual and planned carefully"
- KC Fed's Schmid: Inflation remains "the most pressing risk"
- 10-year Treasury at 4.44% — Schwab's Cooper Howard: "Yields likely to remain elevated across the curve" on fiscal sustainability + inflation concerns

**Implication:** The balance sheet debate creates a new source of policy uncertainty. Markets are pricing rate hikes by March 2027, but the regime change could tighten financial conditions through channels the market doesn't fully understand yet.

## Oil & Geopolitics: The Peace Premium

Brent crude's 19% May collapse to $92.05 is the biggest monthly drop since the COVID crash of March 2020. The catalyst: Treasury Secretary Bessent said U.S. and Iran have "the makings of a deal."

Trump's Truth Social post Friday morning — "meeting with my team to make a final determination" — sent oil down another 1.7% on the day. White House demands: Iran must dispose of enriched uranium, renounce nuclear weapons, and fully reopen the Strait of Hormuz.

**What's priced in vs. what's not:**
- Priced in: Deal optimism, reduced supply disruption risk, reopening of Hormuz
- NOT priced in: Deal collapse, renewed escalation, or sanctions snapback
- The asymmetry: oil has further to fall on a deal than to rise on failure, because the 19% drop already reflects 80% probability

For equities, falling oil is unambiguously disinflationary — it eases the Fed's energy-shock dilemma, reduces input costs, and supports consumer spending. This is the single biggest macro tailwind of the week.

## Credit: The Cracks Beneath the Surface

Private credit — the $1.7T market that financed the leveraged buyout boom — is entering its first real stress test since 2008.

From the With Intelligence deep-dive:
- **"True" default rate near 5%** (headline rate <2% masks selective defaults and liability management)
- **PIK income now 8%** of BDC investment income — borrowers paying interest with more debt
- **40% of private-credit borrowers have negative free cash flow** (IMF), up from 25% in 2021
- **$100B+ of opportunistic/distressed funds** are being raised to buy the coming distressed debt

But there's a structural shift happening too:
- **European private credit fundraising hit record $65B** (first 9 months 2025), up 14% from 2024's full year
- US allocators diversifying away from commoditized US direct lending
- **Evergreen fund AuM: $644B** — perpetual capital vehicles now dominate the industry
- Liquidity concerns brewing: Blue Owl's abandoned OBDC II → OBDC merger attempt signals structural issues in non-traded BDC space

**The tension:** Credit stress is building slowly (not a crisis), but the firepower to buy distressed assets ($100B+) is enormous. This sets up a 2026-2027 credit opportunity cycle — the question is timing, not direction.
