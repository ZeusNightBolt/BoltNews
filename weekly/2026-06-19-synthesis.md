## Macro and Policy Outlook

### The Warsh Doctrine — A Structural Break

Kevin Warsh's first FOMC meeting was the most consequential Fed communication shift since the 2012 adoption of the dot plot. Key elements:

1. **Forward guidance eliminated.** "I can't give you any forward guidance about what we're going to do next. The good news is we'll be meeting in six weeks." This returns the Fed to pre-2012 opacity — markets must now price policy based entirely on data, not Fed signals.

2. **Dot plot restructured.** Warsh declined to submit his own projection — unprecedented for a Fed Chair. The dots that were submitted showed 9/18 participants projecting at least one hike in 2026 (zero in March). The median year-end rate jumped from 3.4% to 3.8%.

3. **Inflation forecast revised sharply higher.** End-2026 PCE now seen at 3.6% (from 2.7% in March), driven by Iran war energy disruptions. 17/18 participants assess inflation uncertainty as "higher" than the past 20-year average.

4. **Five task forces** will review communications, balance sheet (QT), data sources, productivity/AI, and inflation frameworks. Findings expected by year-end. This could produce further structural changes before the 2027 FOMC cycle.

5. **Statement shortened.** "Dispenses with some older language … gives you the facts as best we can judge it." The statement no longer includes easing-bias language.

## Contrarian Flags and Underpriced Risks

### 1. The Intel-Apple Deal Is Unconfirmed — and the Entire Rally Rests on It
Trump's Truth Social post is the sole source for a claimed Apple-Intel chip fabrication partnership. **Neither Apple nor Intel has confirmed it.** Intel declined to comment on "a potential Apple–Intel agreement." If denied or walked back over the 3-day weekend, INTC could give back its entire 11% Thursday gain — and the semiconductor sector's +3% Wednesday divergence and Thursday's broad rally with it. This is a single-point-of-failure risk hiding in plain sight.

### 2. The Dollar at a One-Year High + Equities Near Records Is Historically Fragile
DXY 101.13 with S&P 500 at 7,500 is an unusual correlation. Historically, a dollar this strong pressures EM, commodities, and multinational earnings. The correlation tends to break in one of two ways: either stocks correct or the dollar reverses. The June 29 PCE print is the most likely catalyst.

### 3. Markets Are Pricing the Hawkish FOMC but Not the Disinflationary Tail Risk
Warsh's dot plot assumes persistent energy disruption (3.6% PCE). If the Iran deal holds through the 60-day negotiation period and the Strait of Hormuz stays open, oil could drift toward $70. That would collapse the inflation impulse and make the September hike unnecessary. The market is pricing 90% hike probability but 0% chance of a dovish reversal. That asymmetry favors duration-sensitive assets (bonds, gold, growth stocks).

### 4. VIX at 16.88 Prices No Sustained Uncertainty After a Regime-Shift Fed Meeting
A new Fed Chair just eliminated forward guidance, 9/18 dots project hikes, and Iran follow-up talks were postponed — yet the VIX is below 17. This is aggressive vol compression. Any data surprise (PCE, geopolitical) hits a market with minimal hedges.

### 5. Accenture's -16.5% Is a Canary for AI's Creative Destruction
The market narrative is "AI is an everything-bullish capex cycle." But ACN's revenue miss and guidance cut with Morgan Stanley citing "AI spending crowding out IT services budgets" tells a different story: AI is destroying some business models even as it creates others. If this pattern spreads to other IT services firms (IBM, Cognizant, Infosys), the rotation out of software/services could accelerate.

### 6. SpaceX's Float Dynamics Are a Convexity Time Bomb
4.2% public float. $60B acquisition. $20B+ bond offering. Index inclusion Monday. Retail flows evaporated Thursday ($9.1M vs $300M+). If passive buying disappoints or early investors use the inclusion event to sell, the unwind could be violent. Conversely, forced buying on a tiny float could produce a squeeze.
